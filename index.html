Bo Gets Squished

Bo starts in a container with lots of space, then the container gets smaller.

When space gets tight, gas particles hit the walls more. That means pressure goes up.

Equation Page

Name of gas law: Boyle's Law

P1V1 = P2V2

Concept: Pressure and volume go opposite ways when temperature stays the same.

Labeled example

Before: P1 = 1.0 atm, V1 = 4.0 L

After: V2 = 2.0 L, so P2 = 2.0 atm

Slide 5 James Watson Charles' Law

Char Heats Up

When Char gets warmer, particles move faster and spread out more.

So the balloon gets bigger if pressure stays the same.

Kid version: Warm air moves more, so it needs more room.

Temperature must be in Kelvin.

Diagram

Cold balloon = smaller volume
Heat up
Warm balloon = bigger volume

Temperature up, volume up.

Slide 6 James Watson Charles' Law

Equation Page

Name of gas law: Charles' Law

V1/T1 = V2/T2 (T in Kelvin)

Concept: Volume and temperature go up together when pressure stays constant.

Labeled example

Before: V1 = 1.5 L at T1 = 300 K

After: T2 = 450 K, so V2 = 2.25 L

Gigi in a Sealed Can

Gigi is in a rigid can, so volume cannot change.

When it gets hotter, particles hit the walls harder, so pressure goes up.

Kid version: Same room size, faster runners, stronger wall hits.

Constant: volume and amount of gas

Slide 10 James Watson Combined Gas Law

Equation Page

Name of gas law: Combined Gas Law

(P1V1)/T1 = (P2V2)/T2

Concept: The before-and-after values stay balanced if the gas amount is constant.

Labeled example

Before: P1 = 1.2 atm, V1 = 3.0 L, T1 = 300 K

After: P2 = 1.0 atm, T2 = 250 K, solve V2 = 2.08 L

Slide 11 James Watson

Appendix: Sample Calculations (Part 1)

1) Boyle's Law

Given: P1 = 1.5 atm, V1 = 4.0 L, V2 = 2.0 L. Find P2.

P1V1 = P2V2 (1.5)(4.0) = P2(2.0) 6.0 = 2.0P2 P2 = 3.0 atm

2) Charles' Law

Given: V1 = 1.2 L, T1 = 300 K, T2 = 450 K. Find V2.

V1/T1 = V2/T2 1.2/300 = V2/450 V2 = (1.2 x 450)/300 V2 = 1.8 L
Slide 12 James Watson

Appendix: Sample Calculations (Part 2)

3) Gay-Lussac's Law

Given: P1 = 95 kPa, T1 = 280 K, T2 = 350 K. Find P2.

P1/T1 = P2/T2 95/280 = P2/350 P2 = (95 x 350)/280 P2 = 118.8 kPa

4) Combined Gas Law

Given: P1 = 1.0 atm, V1 = 2.5 L, T1 = 300 K, P2 = 1.2 atm, T2 = 330 K. Find V2.

(P1V1)/T1 = (P2V2)/T2 (1.0 x 2.5)/300 = (1.2 x V2)/330 (2.5 x 330)/300 = 1.2V2 2.75 = 1.2V2 V2 = 2.29 L

Glossary (Part 1)

Boyle's Law: Pressure and volume change opposite ways when temperature is constant. (p.3, p.4, p.11)
Charles' Law: Volume and temperature change together when pressure is constant. (p.5, p.6, p.11)
Gay-Lussac's Law: Pressure and temperature change together when volume is constant. (p.7, p.8, p.12)
Combined Gas Law: One law that connects pressure, volume, and temperature at once. (p.9, p.10, p.12)
Pressure (P): How hard gas particles push on the walls of a container. (p.3, p.4, p.7, p.8)
Volume (V): The amount of space gas takes up. (p.3, p.5, p.6, p.9)
Gas Particles: Tiny pieces of gas that move around and bump into each other and container walls. (p.3, p.5, p.7)
Constant: A value that stays the same while other values change. (p.3, p.7, p.10)
Slide 14 James Watson

Glossary (Part 2)

Temperature (T): A measure of how hot or cold something is. (p.5, p.6, p.8, p.10)
Kelvin (K): The temperature scale used in gas law equations. (p.5, p.6, p.8, p.11)
Atmosphere (atm): A pressure unit. (p.4, p.10, p.11)
kPa: Kilopascal, another pressure unit. (p.8, p.12)
Amount of Gas: How much gas is in the container. (p.3, p.7, p.9)
Inverse Relationship: When one variable goes up while the other goes down. (p.3, p.4)
Direct Relationship: When two variables increase or decrease together. (p.5, p.6, p.8)
Rigid Container: A container that cannot expand, so volume stays fixed. (p.7, p.8)
